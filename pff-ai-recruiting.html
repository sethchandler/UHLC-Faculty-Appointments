Non-Traditional Sourcing Guide

For the PFF Position in Artificial Intelligence & Law

Key Postdoctoral & Fellowship Programs

These programs are well-known incubators for the next generation of technology law scholars. Reviewing their current fellows and recent alumni could provide a list of exceptionally well-qualified candidates.

Yale Law School

Information Society Project (ISP)

One of the most prestigious fellowships, hosting a mix of J.D.s and Ph.D.s working at the intersection of law, technology, and society.

Princeton University

Center for Information Technology Policy (CITP)

Brings together technologists, social scientists, and legal scholars. Fellows often produce cutting-edge work on AI governance.

Stanford Law School

Center for Internet and Society (CIS)

A long-standing leader with a track record of producing excellent scholars. Their residential and non-residential fellows are worth a close look.

NYU School of Law

Information Law Institute (ILI)

Sponsors fellows and hosts events that attract leading thinkers on data, privacy, and AI. Their network is a rich source of potential candidates.

Cornell Tech

Digital Life Initiative (DLI)

Focused on ethics, policy, and quality of life in digital society. Postdoctoral fellows are often engaged in directly relevant research.

Interdisciplinary Academic Hubs

To find a truly interdisciplinary candidate, we can look at graduates and junior faculty from these leading non-law school programs and conferences.

These departments blend social science and computer science. Their Ph.D. graduates often have the technical and policy-oriented background we are seeking.

  • UC Berkeley School of Information
  • University of Michigan School of Information
  • University of Washington Information School
  • Carnegie Mellon's School of Computer Science (societal computing)

These programs specialize in the societal implications of technology. We may find candidates with a J.D. who have pursued a Ph.D. in this area.

  • MIT
  • Cornell
  • UC Davis

Key Academic Conferences

The ACM Conference on Fairness, Accountability, and Transparency (FAccT) is the premier academic venue for this type of research. Reviewing proceedings from the last few years could yield a list of dozens of potential candidates, including junior scholars and Ph.D. students on the verge of graduating.

Talent from Industry & Public Interest

Some of the most knowledgeable experts on how AI is "built, deployed, and governed" are currently working outside of academia. These channels can help us find them.

Many tech companies have dedicated teams working on responsible AI. Exploring their staff could reveal individuals with strong academic backgrounds who may be interested in moving to a faculty position.

  • Microsoft Research: FATE (Fairness, Accountability, Transparency, and Ethics in AI) group.
  • Google: Responsible AI and Human-Centered Technology (RAI-HCT) organization.
  • Other sources: Authors of policy papers and blog posts from companies like OpenAI, Anthropic, and Meta.

These organizations are often staffed by J.D.s and Ph.D.s doing policy-relevant research.

  • The AI Now Institute: A leading research center on the social implications of AI.
  • Center for Democracy & Technology (CDT): Teams working on AI governance and privacy are excellent.
  • EFF & EPIC: Staff attorneys and technologists with deep expertise in legal/constitutional questions raised by AI.
